## Exercise 1: Brainstorming expansion

### Theoretical background

This exercise demonstrates the power of AI to amplify human creativity beyond normal cognitive limits. Research shows that most humans plateau at 8-12 unique ideas during solo brainstorming sessions due to cognitive load and fixation on initial concepts. AI tools can break through these mental barriers by introducing novel associations, challenging assumptions, and maintaining consistent creative momentum without fatigue.

The contrast between unaided and AI-assisted brainstorming highlights a key AI capability: augmenting rather than replacing human thought. The initial unassisted phase establishes your baseline creativity, while the AI-assisted phase demonstrates how external prompting can unlock lateral thinking and unconventional applications.

### Student task

First, spend 5 minutes brainstorming 10 ways to use a brick without any AI assistance. Write them down. Next, open your AI tool and ask it to help you generate 20 different ways to use a brick. Compare the quality, diversity, and creativity of both lists.

Reflect on which ideas you would never have thought of independently and which categories of uses (practical, artistic, metaphorical) were expanded most by AI assistance.

### Examples

**Without AI (typical responses):**
1. Build a wall
2. Doorstop
3. Paperweight
4. Weapon for self-defense
5. Garden border
6. Exercise weight
7. Break a window in emergency
8. Prop for furniture
9. Heat absorber for oven
10. Grind into powder for art

**With AI (expanded responses):**
11. pH neutralizer for acidic soil
12. Heat sink for electronics projects
13. Percussion instrument for experimental music
14. Thermal mass for passive solar heating
15. Mold for casting concrete shapes
16. Abrasive surface for sharpening tools
17. Anchor point for outdoor shade structures
18. Educational prop for physics demonstrations
19. Color pigment source (crushed red brick)
20. Moisture regulator in terrariums

### Gotchas

Don't accept AI's first response passively. Challenge it to explore specific categories (scientific uses, artistic applications, industrial purposes) to push beyond generic answers. Some AI responses may be impractical or physically impossible, requiring your judgment to filter.

Beware of "idea inflation" where the AI generates more ideas but not necessarily better ones. Quality and originality matter more than quantity.

### Key takeaways

AI excels at pattern matching across vast knowledge domains, connecting concepts you might never associate independently. The best results come from combining your domain expertise and judgment with AI's broad associative capabilities. This exercise establishes that AI is a thought partner, not a replacement for human creativity.

## Exercise 2: Self-assessment essay writing

### Theoretical background

This exercise teaches structured self-reflection using AI as a framework generator. The Socratic method of learning through questioning is one of the oldest and most effective pedagogical approaches. AI can generate targeted questions that stimulate deeper introspection than you might achieve through freeform writing.

The technique of having AI generate guiding questions before writing helps overcome the "blank page problem" and ensures comprehensive coverage of a topic. By externalizing the structure-creation process, you free cognitive resources to focus on genuine reflection rather than organizational decisions.

### Student task

Ask AI to generate 10 questions about how you learn best. Answer these questions honestly in unstructured form (3 minutes of freeform writing). Then ask AI to correct and organize your writing into a coherent essay. Next, have the AI quiz you on your own learning style to test if you truly understand it. If you fail the quiz, you failed the exercise.

Finally, convert your essay into a poem in 3 languages you know, demonstrating how AI can transform content across formats and languages.

### Examples

**AI-generated questions:**
1. What time of day are you most focused and receptive to new information?
2. Do you prefer learning through reading, listening, watching, or doing?
3. How important is social interaction to your learning process?
4. What role does immediate feedback play in your retention?
5. How do you handle frustration when encountering difficult concepts?
6. What environmental factors (noise, lighting, setting) optimize your learning?
7. Do you learn better with structured curricula or self-directed exploration?
8. How long can you maintain deep focus before needing a break?
9. What memory techniques or study habits have proven most effective for you?
10. How do you measure whether you've truly learned something?

**Unstructured response example:**
"I think I learn best in the morning when I'm fresh, but sometimes I get distracted easily. I like reading but also need to practice things hands-on or I forget them. I don't like strict curricula because I want to explore tangents that interest me. I need quiet environments..."

**AI-corrected essay example:**
"My optimal learning occurs during morning hours when cognitive resources are fresh and distractions minimal. While I value reading as an initial exposure method, true retention requires hands-on application and practice. I thrive in self-directed learning environments that permit exploration of tangential interests, though this preference sometimes conflicts with structured curricula. Environmental factors significantly impact my focus, with quiet settings proving essential for deep work..."

### Gotchas

The AI quiz may be too easy if the AI simply regurgitates your essay content. Explicitly instruct it to test application and synthesis, not just recall. If you find yourself copying the AI's corrections verbatim, you're losing the learning opportunity—engage with why the AI's version is better.

The multilingual poem exercise may produce grammatically correct but culturally awkward results. AI translation and creative writing across languages remains imperfect, especially for idioms and cultural references.

### Key takeaways

AI can scaffold learning about yourself by providing structure where you might struggle. The act of having AI quiz you on your own writing forces you to internalize rather than simply produce content. This exercise demonstrates AI's role in both content generation and personalized assessment.

## Exercise 3: Multilingual creative transformation

### Theoretical background

This exercise explores AI's capacity for cross-format and cross-linguistic transformation. Translation is not merely word substitution but cultural and contextual adaptation. Similarly, transforming prose into poetry requires understanding rhythm, metaphor, and emotional resonance. AI models trained on multilingual corpora can perform these transformations while maintaining semantic core meaning.

This exercise builds on Exercise 2 by taking the self-assessment essay and transforming it into poetry across three languages. It demonstrates how AI can preserve meaning while adapting form, meter, and cultural context.

### Student task

Take your learning style essay from Exercise 2. Ask AI to convert it into a poem in three languages you know. For each language, specify the desired poetic style (haiku, sonnet, free verse, etc.). Evaluate whether the poems maintain your original meaning while successfully adapting to each language's cultural and linguistic characteristics.

If you don't know three languages, use three different poetic styles in your native language instead.

### Examples

**Original essay excerpt:**
"My optimal learning occurs during morning hours when cognitive resources are fresh and distractions minimal."

**English (Haiku):**
Morning mind awakens
Fresh thoughts flow uninterrupted
Knowledge takes deep root

**Spanish (Free verse):**
En las horas tempranas, cuando la mente despierta,
los recursos cognitivos florecen sin distracción,
y el aprendizaje encuentra su momento perfecto.

**French (Rhyming couplet):**
Au matin quand l'esprit s'éveille sans contrainte,
L'apprentissage optimal commence sans plainte.

### Gotchas

AI may prioritize poetic form over meaning preservation, especially in structured forms like haiku or sonnets. Check that core ideas survive transformation. Rhyme and meter requirements in some languages may force awkward word choices or loss of nuance.

AI translations into languages you don't speak fluently cannot be verified for cultural appropriateness. If sharing such work, disclose that it's AI-generated and unverified by native speakers.

### Key takeaways

AI can bridge linguistic and creative divides, though human judgment remains essential for quality control. This exercise reveals both AI's impressive cross-linguistic capabilities and its limitations in capturing cultural subtleties and poetic authenticity.

## Exercise 4: AI mentor role-playing

### Theoretical background

Role prompting leverages AI's ability to adopt specific personas and perspectives by accessing pattern-matched behavior from its training data. When you ask AI to assume a mentor's identity, it draws on textual patterns associated with mentorship, coaching, and advising. This technique is particularly powerful for gaining external perspective on your own decisions and plans.

The psychological benefit mirrors the "rubber duck debugging" technique in programming—articulating your situation to an external entity (even a simulated one) often clarifies your own thinking. AI mentors can challenge assumptions and offer alternative viewpoints without the social dynamics that sometimes inhibit human mentoring relationships.

### Student task

Ask AI to take the role of your mentor or a specific person who inspires you (provide context about who they are and why you admire them). Describe what you did last week in detail. Ask for feedback on your activities, priorities, and outcomes.

Engage in at least three rounds of follow-up questions to drill deeper into specific feedback points.

### Examples

**Student prompt:**
"Act as Cal Newport, author of 'Deep Work' and expert on focused productivity. I'm inspired by your emphasis on deep work and distraction-free environments. Here's what I did last week: I worked on three client projects simultaneously, attended 12 meetings, responded to 200+ emails, and spent about 30 minutes total in focused deep work. I feel busy but unaccomplished. What's your feedback?"

**AI response (as Cal Newport):**
"Your week illustrates a common modern knowledge work trap: confusing activity with productivity. Thirty minutes of deep work across an entire week is alarming—that's essentially zero. Twelve meetings suggest you're operating in what I call 'shallow work mode,' where you're perpetually available and responsive but never building the deep focus required for meaningful intellectual output.

The simultaneous project juggling guarantees context-switching costs that devastate cognitive efficiency. Each time you shift between projects, you incur an 'attention residue' tax where part of your mind remains engaged with the previous task. Three parallel projects might make you feel productive, but you're likely operating at 40-50% of your potential effectiveness on each.

Let's start with a hard question: Which of those three projects genuinely requires your unique expertise, and which could be delegated, delayed, or declined entirely?"

**Follow-up drilling:**
Student: "All three projects require my expertise because I'm the only senior developer. How do I create deep work time when clients expect quick responses?"

AI (as Cal Newport): "This is a false constraint you're accepting as immutable. Being the only senior developer is a systems problem your organization needs to solve, not a permanent condition you must accommodate..."

### Gotchas

AI mentors lack genuine personal investment in your success and cannot replace human mentoring relationships. They also cannot access information about you beyond what you provide in the conversation. If you're seeking validation rather than honest feedback, you may inadvertently prompt AI to confirm your biases.

The AI cannot truly embody another person's complete worldview, only pattern-match their public persona and known positions. Don't treat its responses as what that person would definitely say, but rather as "in the style of" that perspective.

### Key takeaways

AI role-playing creates psychological distance that enables more honest self-assessment. The technique is most valuable when you actively challenge the AI's feedback and engage in multi-turn dialogue rather than accepting surface-level advice. This sets up the next exercise on critical feedback sessions.

## Exercise 5: Critical feedback sessions

### Theoretical background

This exercise extends Exercise 4 by specifically requesting critical rather than supportive feedback. Research in organizational psychology shows that the most valuable feedback is often the hardest to hear and the least likely to be given in workplace contexts due to social costs. AI mentors face no such social inhibition and can deliver harsh truths without relationship damage.

The technique of asking AI to criticize your plans for the next week, month, and year creates increasing levels of strategic thinking. Weekly plans involve tactical execution, monthly plans require prioritization across competing goals, and yearly plans demand strategic vision. Having AI challenge all three levels reveals blind spots at different time horizons.

### Student task

Continuing from Exercise 4, share your plans for the next week, month, and year. Explicitly instruct the AI mentor to be critical and identify flaws, unrealistic assumptions, and strategic misalignments. After receiving feedback, identify what you don't like about the feedback but acknowledge its relevance. Finally, ask AI to summarize the session and distill key points of disagreement between your perspective and the mentor's.

### Examples

**Student prompt:**
"Here are my plans: This week, I'll complete the three client projects and start two new ones. This month, I'll increase billable hours by 30% while also learning Rust. This year, I'll become a senior architect, start a side business, and maintain perfect work-life balance. Please criticize these plans aggressively."

**AI response (as critical mentor):**
"Your plans are a masterclass in self-sabotage disguised as ambition. Let's dissect them:

This week: You haven't finished three projects yet, and you're already committing to two more? This is the definition of insanity. You're treating symptom (unfinished projects) with more disease (additional projects).

This month: A 30% billable hour increase while learning a new systems programming language is mathematically implausible unless you plan to eliminate sleep. Which of these goals is actually important to you? Because you're guaranteeing you'll fail at both by attempting them simultaneously.

This year: 'Senior architect,' 'side business,' and 'perfect work-life balance' are three mutually exclusive goals for someone currently drowning in client work. You're describing three different life trajectories and claiming you'll walk all three paths simultaneously. Which identity do you actually want?"

**Student response:**
"I don't like being told my goals are unrealistic—I pride myself on being ambitious. But I acknowledge that I've consistently overcommitted in the past and I'm probably doing it again. The work-life balance goal especially feels like wishful thinking given my actual behavior."

**AI summary of disagreement:**
"Core disagreement: You believe ambition means maximizing commitments across all life domains simultaneously. The mentor's perspective is that true ambition means strategic focus on the few objectives that matter most, executed with excellence. Your approach treats goals as accumulative, while the mentor's treats them as competitive for finite resources (time, energy, attention). You value appearing busy and multi-talented; the mentor values deep impact in chosen domains."

### Gotchas

AI critical feedback can be demotivating if you're seeking encouragement during a difficult period. Be mindful of your emotional state when soliciting harsh criticism. Some AI responses may be excessively harsh without being proportionally insightful—not all criticism is useful criticism.

You may unconsciously present your plans in ways that invite specific criticisms you're already aware of, avoiding your actual blind spots.

### Key takeaways

AI can serve as a consequence-free environment for reality testing your plans against critical scrutiny. The distillation of disagreement points is particularly valuable, as it surfaces your implicit assumptions and decision-making frameworks. This exercise builds meta-cognitive awareness about how you think about planning and goal-setting.

## Exercise 6: Complex multi-step problem solving

### Theoretical background

This exercise applies AI to authentic problems from your life, testing its utility beyond theoretical scenarios. Complex problems typically involve multiple variables, competing constraints, uncertain information, and no single "correct" answer. They require both analytical decomposition (breaking the problem into sub-problems) and synthetic reasoning (integrating solutions into a coherent whole).

Using AI to solve such problems reveals both its analytical capabilities and limitations. The validation step—having another AI check the first AI's work, or checking manually—teaches critical evaluation of AI outputs rather than passive acceptance.

### Student task

Identify a complex multi-variable problem from your home life or hobby. Ideally, this problem involves at least 3-5 interdependent factors and has real stakes. Present it to AI with relevant context and constraints. Ask AI to develop a solution. Then, either present the problem and AI's solution to a second AI for validation, or manually check the solution's logic and completeness yourself.

Document where the AI solution was strong, where it was weak, and what human judgment added.

### Examples

**Student problem:**
"I'm planning a 2-week cycling trip across 300km of variable terrain. I need to: (1) budget for accommodation ranging from camping to hostels, (2) plan daily distances accounting for my fitness level (can sustain 60km on flat terrain but only 30km in mountains), (3) factor in weather patterns for the region in June, (4) identify bike repair shops along the route, (5) balance scenic detours against schedule constraints, and (6) coordinate with two friends with different fitness levels. Budget is $800 total. What's the optimal route and daily plan?"

**AI Solution (excerpt):**
"Day 1: Start at Point A, ride 45km to Town B (mixed terrain, 400m elevation gain). Budget: $30 camping. Rationale: Easy first day to assess group fitness without overcommitting.

Day 2: Town B to City C, 65km (flat). Budget: $60 hostel. Rationale: Long flat day to cover distance; hostel allows rest and bike maintenance. City C has two bike shops.

[continues for 14 days with daily breakdown]

Weather note: June historically has 40% rain probability in the mountain section (Days 8-10). Build buffer day after Day 10 for weather delays or fatigue. Total distance: 310km including scenic detours. Budget: $780, with $20 emergency reserve."

**Second AI validation:**
"The solution is generally sound but has a critical flaw: Day 7 proposes 70km with 800m elevation gain, which exceeds your stated mountain capability of 30km. This would likely cause exhaustion affecting subsequent days. Recommendation: Split Day 7 into two shorter days, requiring adjustment to accommodation budget. The weather analysis is appropriately conservative. The placement of the rest day after the mountain section is strategically correct."

**Human addition:**
"Both AIs missed that one friend has a knee injury requiring daily icing. This constrains accommodation choices to places with freezer access, eliminating some camping days. Neither AI asked about this constraint during problem definition."

### Gotchas

AI solutions may be logically consistent but practically infeasible due to unconsidered real-world factors. Always validate whether AI's assumptions match ground truth. AIs cannot account for tacit knowledge you haven't articulated (like the friend's injury in the example).

When using a second AI for validation, you may encounter AI-to-AI bias where the second AI defaults to agreeing with the first. Explicitly instruct the validator to be adversarial.

### Key takeaways

Complex problem-solving with AI is most effective when you treat the AI as a reasoning partner that requires your oversight, not an autonomous solver. The validation step is not optional—it's where the real learning happens as you adjudicate between AI logic and ground reality.

## Exercise 7: Parallel deep research

### Theoretical background

This exercise demonstrates AI's capacity for concurrent research across multiple topics, a task that would require sequential attention from a human researcher. Traditional research is inherently serial—you investigate one topic, document findings, then move to the next. AI research tools can parallelize this process, dramatically compressing research timelines.

The technique teaches efficient information gathering, synthesis across domains, and comparative analysis. By researching 10 topics simultaneously, you learn to formulate clear research questions, evaluate source quality in AI summaries, and identify patterns across diverse subjects.

### Student task

Identify 10 topics you're curious about, spanning different domains (technology, history, science, arts, business, etc.). These should be substantial topics requiring synthesis of multiple sources, not simple factual lookups. Use a deep research tool capable of parallel processing to investigate all 10 simultaneously. Compare the depth and quality of results across topics. Identify which topics benefited most from AI research and which required more human curation.

### Examples

**10 research topics:**
1. The economic impact of remote work on urban commercial real estate (2020-2024)
2. Comparative analysis of microbiome influence on mental health across different dietary patterns
3. Evolution of jazz improvisation techniques from bebop to modern fusion
4. Game theory applications in cryptocurrency protocol design
5. Archaeological evidence for pre-Columbian trans-Pacific contact
6. Neuroplasticity and adult language acquisition after age 40
7. Supply chain resilience strategies in semiconductor manufacturing
8. The role of mycorrhizal networks in forest ecosystem communication
9. Behavioral economics insights on workplace productivity incentives
10. Technical architecture of quantum error correction in trapped ion systems

**Parallel research insights:**
- Topics 1, 4, and 7 benefited from AI's ability to synthesize recent news, academic papers, and industry reports released in the past 2-3 years
- Topic 5 required careful evaluation of source credibility, as AI surfaced both peer-reviewed archaeological evidence and fringe theories without clear differentiation
- Topic 10 (quantum computing) produced highly technical explanations that required domain expertise to verify accuracy
- Topic 3 (jazz) was surprisingly strong, with AI connecting musical evolution to cultural and technological changes

### Gotchas

Parallel research quality varies dramatically by topic. Well-documented subjects with abundant digital sources yield better results than niche topics with limited online presence. AI may conflate correlation with causation in complex topics or present theoretical frameworks as established fact.

Research depth is limited by your prompt specificity. Vague questions like "tell me about X" produce shallow summaries. Specific questions like "what are the three main competing theoretical frameworks for X, and what evidence supports each?" produce better results.

### Key takeaways

Parallel AI research is most powerful for initial topic exploration and literature mapping, not deep expertise development. Use it to identify promising areas for human deep-dive research. The technique reveals how AI can compress reconnaissance-level learning from weeks to hours.

## Exercise 8: Study notes creation

### Theoretical background

This exercise builds directly on Exercise 7 by transforming raw research output into structured study materials. The process of converting information into notes is not mere transcription but active learning through organization, summarization, and connection-making. AI can accelerate this process while you maintain control over structure and emphasis.

Effective study notes balance comprehensiveness with conciseness, hierarchical organization with cross-referencing, and technical accuracy with accessible explanations. AI can generate initial note structures that you then refine based on your learning goals.

### Student task

Take the research results from Exercise 7's 10 topics. Ask AI to create study notes for each topic in a consistent format. Specify the desired structure (e.g., key concepts, main arguments, supporting evidence, open questions, connections to other topics). Review the notes for accuracy, clarity, and usefulness. Identify where AI summaries lost important nuance or where you need to supplement with additional detail.

### Examples

**Study notes structure template:**
```
# Topic: [Name]

## Key concepts
- [3-5 fundamental ideas or terms]

## Main arguments/findings
- [2-4 central claims or discoveries]

## Supporting evidence
- [Sources, studies, examples]

## Critical questions/debates
- [Unresolved issues or competing perspectives]

## Connections to other topics
- [Links to other research areas]

## Personal insights
- [Your observations or applications]
```

**Sample output for Topic 6 (Neuroplasticity and adult language learning):**

**Key concepts**
- Neuroplasticity: brain's ability to form new neural connections throughout life
- Critical period hypothesis: traditional view that language acquisition has age-based window
- Hebbian learning: "neurons that fire together wire together"
- Executive function: cognitive control systems that mature with age

**Main arguments/findings**
- Adults can achieve high proficiency in new languages, but through different mechanisms than children
- Adults leverage existing language frameworks and metalinguistic awareness (understanding grammar explicitly)
- Reduced neuroplasticity is partially offset by superior metacognitive strategies
- Accent acquisition is genuinely more difficult for adults due to phonological map establishment in childhood

**Supporting evidence**
- Hartshorne et al. (2018) study of 670,000 language learners showing grammar learning curves flatten after age 17-18, not earlier
- Archila-Suerte et al. (2015) fMRI studies showing adults use left hemisphere analytical regions more than children during language processing
- Anecdotal evidence from diplomatic language programs showing adults achieving B2-C1 proficiency with intensive training

**Critical questions/debates**
- Debate over whether true "native-like" proficiency is achievable after 40, or whether minor deficits always remain
- Role of motivation and practice intensity versus biological constraints
- Individual variation in adult neuroplasticity

**Connections to other topics**
- Links to Topic 2 (microbiome/mental health) through brain-gut axis influence on cognitive function
- Links to jazz improvisation (Topic 3) through parallels in procedural learning and automaticity development

**Personal insights**
- Challenges my assumption that language learning after 40 is futile
- Suggests explicit grammar study might be an advantage for adult learners, not a workaround for inability to acquire naturally
- Raises question: Am I avoiding language learning due to actual limits or internalized age-based expectations?

### Gotchas

AI-generated notes may emphasize easily quantifiable findings over nuanced qualitative insights. Check that important debates, controversies, and limitations of research aren't glossed over in favor of clean conclusions. AI may also create false confidence by presenting tentative findings as established fact.

The "Personal insights" section requires your genuine reflection, not AI generation. Don't let AI write this section for you, as it defeats the learning purpose.

### Key takeaways

Study note creation with AI is a collaborative process where AI handles structural organization and information compression while you provide critical evaluation and personal meaning-making. The notes become more valuable when you iterate on AI's initial draft rather than accepting it as final.

## Exercise 9: Chain of thought prompting

### Theoretical background

Chain of thought (CoT) prompting is a breakthrough technique that dramatically improves AI reasoning, particularly for STEM, logic, and multi-step problems. The method works by explicitly instructing the model to show its reasoning process step-by-step, which forces the model to engage in more deliberate processing rather than pattern-matching to surface-level answers.

Research shows CoT prompting can improve accuracy on complex reasoning tasks by 20-40%. The technique leverages the model's training on human-written explanatory text where people naturally show their work. By requesting this format, you activate more sophisticated reasoning pathways within the model.

### Student task

Select a complex reasoning problem from mathematics, logic, or multi-step analysis. First, ask AI to solve it directly without any CoT prompt. Note the answer. Then, ask the same question with an explicit CoT instruction: "Let's think through this step-by-step..." or "Please show your reasoning process before providing the final answer." Compare the quality, accuracy, and usefulness of both responses.

Try this with at least three different problem types to see where CoT provides most value.

### Examples

**Problem 1: Mathematical reasoning**
"A farmer has 17 sheep. All but 9 die. How many sheep are left?"

**Without CoT:**
AI: "8 sheep remain."
[Incorrect—this is pattern-matching to subtraction]

**With CoT:**
"Let's think through this step-by-step:
1. The farmer starts with 17 sheep
2. The phrase 'all but 9 die' means that 9 sheep did NOT die
3. If 9 sheep did not die, then 9 sheep are still alive
4. The number of sheep that died is 17 - 9 = 8, but the question asks how many are LEFT (alive)
5. Answer: 9 sheep remain alive."
[Correct]

**Problem 2: Logic puzzle**
"Three switches outside a room control three bulbs inside. You can flip switches but can only enter the room once. How do you determine which switch controls which bulb?"

**Without CoT:**
"Flip switches, enter room, observe which bulbs are on."
[Incomplete—misses the constraint]

**With CoT:**
"Let's work through this systematically:
1. Constraint: Only one entry means we can't use trial-and-error testing
2. Insight: Light bulbs produce heat when on, providing a second observable signal beyond light
3. Strategy:
   - Turn on switch 1, wait 5 minutes, turn it off
   - Turn on switch 2
   - Leave switch 3 off
   - Enter room
4. Observation decode:
   - Bulb that's on: controlled by switch 2
   - Bulb that's off but warm: controlled by switch 1
   - Bulb that's off and cool: controlled by switch 3
5. This solves the puzzle in one entry."
[Correct and complete]

**Problem 3: Multi-step analysis**
"A company's revenue increased 50% from Year 1 to Year 2, then decreased 50% from Year 2 to Year 3. What's the net change from Year 1 to Year 3?"

**Without CoT:**
"No change—the increases and decreases cancel out."
[Incorrect—common misconception]

**With CoT:**
"Let's calculate step-by-step with concrete numbers:
1. Assume Year 1 revenue = $100
2. Year 2 = $100 + 50% = $100 × 1.5 = $150
3. Year 3 = $150 - 50% = $150 × 0.5 = $75
4. Net change = $75 - $100 = -$25
5. Percentage change = -25/100 = -25%
6. Result: Revenue decreased by 25% overall, not zero. The percentage changes don't cancel because they apply to different base values."
[Correct]

### Gotchas

CoT doesn't magically fix all reasoning errors—it reduces them but doesn't eliminate them. The AI might show step-by-step reasoning that looks correct but contains subtle flaws. You still need to verify the logic yourself. CoT also increases response length, which may not be desirable for simple questions.

Some models are trained with CoT capabilities built-in and may show reasoning steps even without explicit prompting. The technique is most impactful with models that tend toward terse, direct answers.

### Key takeaways

CoT prompting transforms AI from a answer-generator into a reasoning-partner. The visible reasoning process lets you audit the AI's logic, catch errors early, and understand why it reaches specific conclusions. This technique is essential for any high-stakes reasoning task where accuracy matters more than speed.

## Exercise 10: Few-shot prompting

### Theoretical background

Few-shot prompting teaches AI through examples rather than explicit instructions. This technique leverages the model's ability to recognize patterns and generalize from demonstrations. Research in machine learning shows that 3-5 well-chosen examples can establish complex patterns more effectively than lengthy written instructions, especially for tasks involving specific formats, styles, or domain-specific conventions.

The technique is particularly powerful when you need consistent output formatting (like JSON structures), specific tones, or domain-specific responses. It works because transformer models excel at pattern completion—they're fundamentally prediction engines that continue patterns they recognize.

### Student task

Identify a task where you need AI to produce output in a specific format or style. First, try describing the format in words alone. Then, try the same task using few-shot prompting with 3-5 examples. Compare the accuracy and consistency of outputs. Experiment with edge cases to see if the few-shot approach generalizes beyond your examples.

Try this with at least two different task types: one structural (format-based) and one stylistic (tone-based).

### Examples

**Task 1: Extract structured data from unstructured text**

**Instruction-only prompt:**
"Extract the person's name, age, location, and profession from the text into JSON format."

**Input:** "Hi, I'm Sarah, a 34-year-old graphic designer living in Portland."

**Output (inconsistent):**
```json
{
  "person": "Sarah",
  "age": "34 years old",
  "location": "Portland",
  "job": "graphic designer"
}
```
[Issues: inconsistent key names, redundant "years old" in age value]

**Few-shot prompt:**
"Extract information into JSON format. Examples:

### Theoretical background

Reverse prompting inverts the typical human-asks-AI-answers dynamic by explicitly encouraging AI to ask clarifying questions before responding. This technique addresses a fundamental limitation: AI often proceeds with whatever information you provide, filling gaps with assumptions that may not match your actual context or needs.

By instructing "ask me any questions you need before answering," you force yourself to articulate requirements you might not have realized were relevant. This surfaces implicit assumptions, edge cases, and contextual factors that dramatically improve response quality. The technique is especially valuable for complex, ambiguous, or high-stakes requests.

[Generic advice that may not match user's actual needs]

### Key takeaways

Reverse prompting forces you to be a better problem-framer. The questions AI asks often reveal dimensions of the problem you hadn't consciously considered. This technique is essential for high-stakes decisions where assumptions can be costly. It transforms AI from answer-provider to thought partner in problem clarification.

## Exercise 14: Designing for edge cases

### Theoretical background

Edge case design addresses a fundamental challenge in AI interactions: how should the system behave when inputs are unexpected, ambiguous, or incomplete? Traditional software engineering emphasizes edge case handling ("outs") to ensure reliability. The same principle applies to prompt engineering. By explicitly instructing AI on what to do when uncertain or when inputs don't match expectations, you improve both reliability and diagnostic value.

This technique teaches defensive prompting—anticipating failure modes and designing graceful handling rather than assuming perfect inputs. It's particularly valuable in production workflows where you're processing many inputs and need consistent, auditable behavior.

### Student task

Design a prompt for a task that processes varied inputs (e.g., extracting information from text, categorizing items, answering questions). First, test it with normal cases. Then, deliberately test edge cases: ambiguous inputs, missing information, contradictory data, or out-of-scope requests. Notice where it fails. Revise your prompt to explicitly handle these edge cases, instructing AI to output specific signals (like `<unsure>` tags or "N/A" values) when it encounters problems. Compare reliability before and after edge case design.

### Examples

**Task:** Extract company name, funding amount, and funding stage from news snippets.

**Initial prompt:**
"Extract company name, funding amount, and funding stage from the text into JSON."

**Test case 1 (normal):**

Output: `{"company": "Acme Corp", "amount": "$12M", "stage": "Series A"}`
[Works correctly]

**Test case 2 (edge case—missing amount):**
Input: "Acme Corp announced Series B funding."
Output: `{"company": "Acme Corp", "amount": "undisclosed", "stage": "Series B"}`
[AI invented "undisclosed" without instruction—problematic for data analysis]

**Test case 3 (edge case—ambiguous stage):**

Output: `{"company": "Acme Corp", "amount": "$12M", "stage": "late-stage"}`
[Inconsistent terminology, hard to analyze]

**Test case 4 (edge case—non-funding text):**

Output: `{"company": "Acme Corp", "amount": "N/A", "stage": "N/A"}`
[Acceptable, but should signal that this isn't a funding announcement]

Test case 3: `{"company": "Acme Corp", "amount": "$12M", "stage": "late-stage", "ambiguous": true}`
[Preserves original terminology and flags ambiguity]

Test case 4: `{"type": "not_funding", "company": "Acme Corp"}`
[Correctly identifies non-funding text]

Over-specifying edge cases can create overly complex prompts that are hard to maintain. Focus on the most common and impactful edge cases. AI may not perfectly follow edge case instructions—test thoroughly with real data. You may discover new edge cases you didn't anticipate, requiring prompt updates.

Edge case handling instructions add prompt length, which can affect performance or cost in API-based scenarios. Balance thoroughness with practicality.

### Key takeaways

Designing for edge cases transforms brittle prompts into robust ones suitable for production workflows. The process of identifying potential failure modes improves your understanding of the task itself. This technique is essential when AI outputs feed into downstream processes where bad data has consequences.

## Exercise 15: Reading and evaluating outputs

## Exercise 17: Tree of thought prompting

### Theoretical background

Tree of thought (ToT) prompting is an advanced technique that encourages AI to explore multiple reasoning paths simultaneously, like a chess player considering several possible move sequences. Unlike linear chain-of-thought reasoning (A→B→C), ToT explores branching possibilities (A→B₁/B₂/B₃) and evaluates which paths are most promising before proceeding.

This technique is particularly powerful for creative problems with no single correct answer, strategic planning with multiple viable approaches, and complex decisions where different assumptions lead to different conclusions. Research shows ToT can significantly improve problem-solving on tasks requiring exploration of solution spaces.

### Student task

Select a problem with multiple valid approaches or creative interpretations (design challenge, strategic decision, open-ended brainstorming). Ask AI to solve it using ToT prompting: "Explore at least three different approaches to this problem. For each approach, identify the key assumptions, potential benefits, and likely challenges. Then recommend which approach to pursue and why."

Compare this to a single-path solution. Note whether the exploration of alternatives reveals insights even in the approaches not ultimately chosen.

### Examples

**Problem:** "Design an employee recognition program for a 50-person software company with limited budget ($5,000/year)."

**Linear approach (single path):**
"Create a monthly 'Employee of the Month' program where nominated winners receive a $100 gift card and recognition in company all-hands. This is a proven, simple approach that fits the budget."
[Functional but unexplored]

**Tree of thought approach:**

"Let me explore three different program philosophies:

**Approach 1: Peer-driven micro-recognition**
- **Philosophy:** Many small recognitions are more impactful than few large ones
- **Implementation:** Give each employee $8/month in recognition tokens they can give to colleagues with written appreciation. Tokens convert to gift cards quarterly.
- **Assumptions:** Employees value peer recognition; frequent small acknowledgments build culture better than rare formal awards
- **Benefits:** High participation, culture of mutual appreciation, visible daily reinforcement
- **Challenges:** Requires platform/system; risk of popularity contests rather than merit-based recognition
- **Budget:** $8 × 50 employees × 12 months = $4,800 + $200 platform cost = $5,000

**Approach 2: Skill development rewards**
- **Philosophy:** Best recognition is investment in employee growth
- **Implementation:** Quarterly recognition awards where winners choose from: conference attendance, course stipend, certification exam, or professional book allowance
- **Assumptions:** Employees value growth opportunities; recognition should have lasting impact beyond momentary validation
- **Benefits:** Tangible skill development; employees customize reward to their goals; company benefits from upskilling
- **Challenges:** Quarterly frequency means fewer people recognized; administrative overhead for conference logistics
- **Budget:** ~$500 per winner × 2-3 winners per quarter = $4,000-$6,000, requires budget flexibility

**Approach 3: Impact-based project completion bonuses**
- **Philosophy:** Recognize specific achievements tied to company goals, not personality or likability
- **Implementation:** Teams nominate completed projects that delivered measurable value (shipped feature, cost saving, customer satisfaction improvement). Nominated projects reviewed quarterly, winning teams split $1,000 recognition bonus.
- **Assumptions:** Team recognition is more valuable than individual; tying recognition to concrete impact focuses culture on results
- **Benefits:** Reinforces outcome orientation; team-based reduces politics; clear criteria
- **Challenges:** May favor customer-facing roles over infrastructure/support; quarterly review lag means recognition isn't timely
- **Budget:** $1,000 × 4 quarters = $4,000 + $1,000 for runner-up/honorable mentions = $5,000

**Recommendation:**
Start with Approach 1 (peer-driven micro-recognition) because:
1. Highest participation—every employee engages monthly, not just award winners
2. Culture-building is the primary value, not the gift card amount
3. Real-time recognition (within days) is more psychologically impactful than quarterly awards
4. Low administrative overhead once system is set up

However, blend in element from Approach 3: annually (end of year), dedicate $1,000 to a 'Project of the Year' team award voted on by the company. This addresses the limitation that peer micro-recognition might miss quieter but highly impactful work.

This hybrid approach uses $4,800 + $200 platform = $5,000, maximizes participation, provides both frequent and significant recognition moments, and builds appreciation culture while rewarding concrete impact."

[Multiple perspectives explored, assumptions made explicit, hybrid solution synthesized]

### Gotchas

ToT responses are much longer than linear responses, which may not be justified for simple problems. The technique can lead to "analysis paralysis" where exploration of options delays decision-making. AI may not genuinely evaluate trade-offs; instead, it might generate three variations that are superficially different but conceptually similar.

Explicitly instruct AI to explore genuinely different approaches (not just variations on a theme) to get maximum value from ToT.

### Key takeaways

ToT prompting forces systematic exploration of solution spaces, revealing both obvious and non-obvious approaches. The value often lies in the divergent thinking during exploration, not just the final recommendation. This technique teaches you to consider problems from multiple frameworks before converging on a solution—a valuable meta-cognitive skill beyond AI use.

## Exercise 18: Step-back prompting

### Theoretical background

Step-back prompting is a technique that improves AI responses by first asking a broader, more general question before addressing the specific query. This activates the model's background knowledge and contextual understanding, which then informs a more insightful response to the specific question. The technique leverages how human expertise works: experts don't just know facts; they understand underlying principles and context.

Research shows that asking AI to establish general principles before applying them to specific cases significantly improves reasoning quality, especially on questions requiring domain knowledge, nuanced understanding, or application of theory to practice.

### Student task

Select a specific, detailed question in a domain where general knowledge matters (science, history, strategy, etc.). First, ask AI the specific question directly. Then, use step-back prompting: ask a broader, related question first, then provide that answer as context for your specific question. Compare the depth and quality of responses.

Try this across 2-3 different domains to see where the technique adds most value.

### Examples

**Specific question:** "Why did the Byzantine Empire survive for nearly 1,000 years after the fall of the Western Roman Empire?"

**Direct response:**
"The Byzantine Empire survived due to several factors: strategic location of Constantinople controlling trade routes, strong defensive fortifications, effective diplomacy, flexible military tactics, and preservation of Roman administrative systems. The capital's walls and geography made it nearly impregnable, and Byzantine emperors skillfully played rivals against each other."
[Accurate but somewhat superficial listing of factors]

**Step-back prompting approach:**

**Step 1—Broader question:**
"What are the fundamental factors that determine whether empires survive long-term versus collapse?"

**AI response to broader question:**
"Empire longevity depends on several interdependent factors:

1. **Economic sustainability:** Can the empire generate sufficient resources to fund defense, administration, and infrastructure without depleting its economic base?

2. **Adaptive governance:** Does the political system adapt to changing circumstances, or is it rigid? Successful long-term empires typically balance continuity with flexibility.

3. **Geographic defensibility:** Are borders naturally defensible (mountains, water) or do they require constant expensive military presence?

4. **Legitimacy and identity:** Does the population accept the state's authority through shared identity, religious/ideological commitment, or effective service delivery?

5. **External threat management:** Can the empire diplomatically isolate threats, form strategic alliances, or maintain military superiority without economically exhausting itself?

6. **Elite cohesion vs. fragmentation:** Internal power struggles drain resources and create vulnerabilities; cohesive elites extend empire survival.

7. **Technological/tactical innovation:** Empires that stagnate militarily or administratively become vulnerable to more adaptive competitors.

These factors interact—economic strength enables military defense, which provides security for economic activity. Collapse often occurs when multiple factors deteriorate simultaneously."

**Step 2—Specific question with context:**
"Now, given these fundamental factors of empire longevity, explain why the Byzantine Empire survived nearly 1,000 years after the Western Roman Empire fell."

**Enhanced response:**
"The Byzantine Empire's millennium-long survival maps remarkably well onto the empire longevity factors:

**Economic sustainability:** Constantinople's position controlling Black Sea-Mediterranean trade routes generated immense customs revenue without requiring exploitative taxation of agricultural regions. Unlike the Western Empire, which depended on costly overland supply lines, Byzantium leveraged maritime trade economics.

**Adaptive governance:** The Byzantine system evolved significantly from Roman precedent—incorporating Greek as the administrative language, developing the theme system (military districts with local recruitment), and eventually accepting iconoclasm controversies through councils rather than civil war. This flexibility contrasts with the Western Empire's rigid adherence to increasingly dysfunctional Roman practices.

**Geographic defensibility:** Constantinople's position (sea on three sides, walls on the fourth) meant the empire could survive even when most territory was lost. The Western Empire had indefensible frontiers requiring constant expensive military presence across the Rhine and Danube.

**Legitimacy and identity:** Byzantine emperors cultivated religious legitimacy (crowned by the Patriarch) and Roman imperial continuity simultaneously. Orthodox Christianity provided ideological cohesion the fragmented West lacked. The concept of 'symphony' between church and state created powerful mutual reinforcement.

**External threat management:** Byzantine diplomacy was legendary—subsidizing steppe peoples to attack each other, forming strategic marriages, exploiting rival Islamic caliphates' conflicts. The Western Empire's less sophisticated diplomacy couldn't prevent coalition-building among barbarian groups.

**Elite cohesion:** While Byzantium had palace intrigues, the centralized bureaucracy and capital city prevented the territorial fragmentation that destroyed the West, where regional warlords became independent.

**Innovation:** Greek fire (naval weapon), heavy cataphract cavalry, sophisticated fortification engineering—Byzantium continually adapted militarily. The Western Empire's late-period armies were essentially Germanic mercenaries with Roman branding.

The key insight: Byzantium succeeded by focusing defensive resources on a highly defensible core (Constantinople and Anatolia) with self-sustaining economics, rather than trying to defend the entire Mediterranean periphery as the Western Empire attempted. When multiple factors aligned negatively (Fourth Crusade's sack of Constantinople, Ottoman technological superiority in siege warfare, loss of Anatolian themes), the empire finally collapsed."

[Much deeper analysis connecting specific factors to general principles, revealing causal mechanisms not just correlations]

### Gotchas

Step-back prompting doubles interaction length (two questions instead of one), which may not be justified for simple queries. The broader question must actually be relevant—a poorly chosen step-back question doesn't help and may even confuse the AI. You need domain awareness to formulate good step-back questions.

Some topics don't benefit from this approach—purely factual questions ("When was X invented?") don't need principle-based context.

### Key takeaways

Step-back prompting transforms AI from fact-retrieval mode into principle-application mode, significantly improving reasoning depth. The technique teaches you to think about the general principles underlying specific situations. This metacognitive habit—asking "what general principle applies here?"—is valuable far beyond AI interactions.

## Exercise 19: Strategic advisory style prompting

### Theoretical background

Strategic advisory prompting combines role prompting with high-context business scenarios to generate sophisticated decision-making support. This technique is particularly valuable for business strategy, leadership decisions, and complex resource allocation problems. By specifying not just the question but the relevant constraints, context, and decision criteria, you enable AI to provide analysis comparable to strategic consulting frameworks.

The technique works because modern AI models have been trained on vast amounts of business literature, case studies, and strategic frameworks. By framing your question in the language of strategic analysis, you activate these knowledge patterns.

### Student task

Identify a real strategic decision from your work or studies. Present it to AI with full context: your current situation, constraints, goals, and relevant metrics. Ask for analysis in the style of a specific strategic thinker or framework (e.g., "analyze this as Ray Dalio would," or "apply Blue Ocean Strategy framework"). Compare the sophistication of advice when full context is provided versus a bare question.

### Examples

**Bare question:**
"Should we expand into the European market?"

**Response:** "Expanding into Europe offers growth opportunities but requires market research, regulatory compliance, and localized operations. Consider market potential, competitive landscape, and resource requirements before deciding."
[Generic, non-actionable]

**Strategic advisory prompt:**
"Acting as Ray Dalio, analyze our company's decision to expand into the European market. Consider our current situation: $2M cash position, team of 15 people, 18-month runway, B2B SaaS product with strong product-market fit in the US, $45K MRR growing 15% monthly, single founder with limited international experience. Our current customers are asking for European data residency options. What principles would guide this decision and what systematic approach should we take?"

**Response:**
"Looking at this through my principles-based framework, I see a classic 'opportunity versus survival' tension that requires careful thought.

**Principle 1: Don't risk what you can't afford to lose**
Your 18-month runway is adequate but not abundant. European expansion would consume cash (legal, infrastructure, sales) and founder attention at a critical growth phase. The fundamental question: Would failed European expansion threaten the core US business? If yes, the risk-reward ratio is wrong.

**Principle 2: Listen to the market, but distinguish signals from noise**
Customer requests for European data residency are meaningful signals—they indicate real demand and potential churn risk if unaddressed. However, distinguish between 'nice to have' and 'deal-breaker.' How many customers are you losing specifically due to lack of European presence? If the answer is 'none yet,' this is forward-looking opportunity, not present crisis.

**Principle 3: Build systems, not heroic efforts**
Single-founder international expansion is a recipe for burnout and execution failure. Before considering geographic expansion, you need systems robust enough to operate without your constant involvement. Can your business run for two weeks without your daily input? If not, international expansion is premature.

**Systematic approach:**

**Phase 1: Validate demand (2 months, $10K budget)**
- Survey existing US customers with European operations: Would they pay for EU data residency? How much? When?
- Interview 15-20 European prospects: Is US-based hosting a deal-breaker or a concern?
- Calculate potential: If 20% of current customer base pays 15% premium for EU hosting, does that cover expansion costs?

**Phase 2: Minimal viable expansion (if Phase 1 validates, 4 months, $50K)**
- Infrastructure-first approach: EU AWS region deployment with data residency compliance
- No European sales team yet—serve EU customers from US with EU infrastructure
- Hire part-time European legal/compliance consultant, not full employees
- Success metric: Sign 5 EU-based customers within 4 months

**Phase 3: Scale only after proof (if Phase 2 succeeds, 6+ months, $200K+)**
- Hire European sales lead based in one target market
- Establish EU legal entity only when revenue justifies it
- Build systematic playbook from learnings before expanding to additional countries

**My recommendation: Do Phase 1 immediately, but delay Phase 2 until your runway extends to 24+ months or MRR hits $75K**. The customer signals are real, but your current cash position and single-founder capacity make aggressive international expansion a bet-the-company risk. You can capture this opportunity, but six months from now when your margin of safety is greater.

The failure mode I'm protecting you against: spreading resources too thin during your most critical growth period, leading to mediocre execution in both markets and squandering your current momentum."

[Specific, principle-driven, phased approach with clear decision criteria and risk analysis]

### Gotchas

Strategic advice from AI lacks accountability—it doesn't have skin in the game. Use AI for analytical frameworks and perspective, but don't delegate final judgment. Highly specific role prompts ("as Ray Dalio") may produce caricature responses that miss nuance.

AI can't access confidential information about your market, competitors, or internal capabilities unless you provide it. The quality of strategic advice is capped by the quality of context you provide.

### Key takeaways

Strategic advisory prompting transforms AI into a thought partner for complex decisions by providing rich context and requesting structured analysis. The technique teaches you to frame business problems with appropriate context and constraints, a skill valuable in human strategic discussions as well.

## Exercise 21: Adaptive Graph of Thoughts

### Theoretical background

Adaptive Graph of Thoughts (AGoT) is the 2025 evolution beyond Chain-of-Thought (linear: A→B→C) and Tree of Thought (branching: A→B₁/B₂/B₃). AGoT structures reasoning as a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) — it decomposes a complex problem into interdependent sub-problems, solves them in dependency order, and synthesises results. Crucially, it only expands sub-problems that are actually needed, reducing wasted reasoning.

**Evidence:** With GPT-4o, AGoT achieved +46.2% improvement on the GPQA Diamond scientific reasoning benchmark and +400% on the "Game of 24" math puzzle compared to standard prompting.
**Source:** [arXiv:2502.05078](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.05078), February 2025.

AGoT is most powerful for problems with genuine dependencies — where solving Part B requires the output of Part A, and Part C requires both. In executive contexts: complex project planning, multi-variable strategic decisions, M&A analysis, regulatory risk mapping.

### Student task

Identify a complex problem with clear interdependencies — at least 3-4 sub-problems where solving later ones requires outputs from earlier ones. Write a prompt that explicitly instructs the model to: (1) decompose into sub-problems, (2) specify dependencies, (3) solve in dependency order referencing earlier results, (4) synthesise into a final answer. Compare to a single direct question on the same problem.

### Examples

**Problem:** Planning a major service line expansion for a professional services firm.

**Standard prompt:**
"Should we expand our AI consulting practice into Germany?"

**AI response:** Generic pros/cons list. No dependency structure. Misses that the financial viability question depends on market demand, which depends on competitive landscape, which depends on your differentiation.

**AGoT prompt:**
```
We are a 50-person AI consulting firm based in London, €8M revenue,
considering expanding into Germany. Please solve this using the following approach:

1. Break the decision into independent sub-problems (e.g. market demand,
   competitive landscape, regulatory requirements, financial viability,
   talent acquisition).
2. Specify which sub-problems depend on the outputs of others.
3. Solve each sub-problem sequentially, referencing prior results where needed.
4. Synthesise a final go/no-go recommendation with clear reasoning.
```

**AI response structure (AGoT):**

*Sub-problem 1: Regulatory and market entry requirements* (no dependencies)
→ Germany requires GDPR plus specific BaFin considerations for financial sector clients. Establishes baseline compliance cost of ~€80-120K setup.

*Sub-problem 2: Competitive landscape* (no dependencies)
→ Dominated by Big 4 and Accenture in enterprise; mid-market gap exists for specialist AI firms. Key differentiator needed: industry vertical or model expertise.

*Sub-problem 3: Market demand* (depends on Sub-problem 2)
→ Given mid-market gap identified above: 340+ German mid-cap firms actively investing in AI with no specialist advisor. Estimated addressable market €45M annually at current maturity.

*Sub-problem 4: Financial viability* (depends on Sub-problems 1 and 3)
→ Given €100K compliance setup and €45M addressable market: break-even at 2.2% market share (€1M revenue), achievable in 18 months based on London ramp pattern.

*Sub-problem 5: Talent acquisition* (depends on Sub-problem 4)
→ Given 18-month timeline and Berlin talent pool: hire 2 senior consultants locally before expansion; pipeline exists.

**Final synthesis:** Go — conditional on securing one anchor client before committing to office lease. Recommend 6-month pilot with remote delivery first.

### Gotchas

Poorly specified dependencies produce shallow results — AI may list sub-problems that are all independent and miss the actual causal chain. Spend time identifying real dependencies before writing the prompt. AGoT is overkill for simple decisions; reserve it for genuinely complex, multi-variable problems with stakeholder presentations or board-level consequences.

### Key takeaways

AGoT transforms AI from answer-machine into structured analyst. By making dependencies explicit, you catch logical gaps that linear reasoning misses. For senior leaders, this technique maps directly to how strategic consultants structure complex engagement analyses — and you can now do it in minutes rather than weeks.

## Exercise 22: Confidence-Informed Self-Consistency

### Theoretical background

Standard Self-Consistency (Wang et al. 2022) asks AI to generate multiple reasoning paths and takes the majority answer. Confidence-Informed Self-Consistency (CISC) improves this by asking the model to also rate its confidence in each path, then applying weighted voting — high-confidence paths count more.

**Evidence:** CISC achieves up to 53% reduction in compute cost compared to standard self-consistency while matching or exceeding accuracy, validated across 9 models and 4 datasets.
**Source:** [arXiv:2502.06233](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.06233), ACL 2025 Findings.

For executive use, CISC is the gold standard for high-stakes decisions where you need both multiple perspectives AND a clear ranked recommendation — not just majority vote, but confidence-weighted synthesis.

### Student task

Take a high-stakes decision you are currently facing (investment, hire, strategic pivot). Write a CISC prompt requesting 3-5 distinct reasoning paths, confidence scores for each, and a final conclusion weighted by confidence. Compare to asking for a single answer on the same question.

### Examples

**Standard single-answer prompt:**
"Should we renew our enterprise software contract at the new price (40% increase) or switch vendors?"

**AI response:** Balanced pros/cons. No clear recommendation. Hedged.

**CISC prompt:**
```
We are evaluating whether to renew our CRM contract (Salesforce, 40% price
increase to €180K/year) or migrate to HubSpot (€95K/year, 6-month migration
estimated). Current team: 12 sales reps, 3 years of historical data.

Generate 4 different reasoning paths analysing this decision.
For each path:
- State the core assumption or lens used (e.g. financial, risk, productivity)
- Reach a clear conclusion (renew / migrate)
- Rate your confidence in this path's conclusion: 0-100
- Briefly explain the confidence rating

Then provide a final weighted recommendation, giving more weight to
higher-confidence paths. Flag any path where confidence is below 50 and explain why.
```

**AI response:**

*Path 1 — Financial lens:* Migration saves €85K/year. Payback on migration cost (estimated €40K) in under 6 months. **Conclusion: Migrate. Confidence: 75.** Well-established cost analysis; uncertainty around migration timeline accuracy.

*Path 2 — Risk/disruption lens:* 6-month migration disrupts Q3 sales cycle. Historical data migration carries 15% risk of CRM data loss without specialist support. **Conclusion: Renew. Confidence: 60.** Disruption cost is real but hard to quantify precisely.

*Path 3 — Productivity lens:* HubSpot's interface shows higher rep adoption in comparable firms; Salesforce has low voluntary usage in your team size range. **Conclusion: Migrate. Confidence: 55.** Productivity gains are real but team-specific; your reps may differ.

*Path 4 — Strategic lock-in lens:* Salesforce's ecosystem integrations with your ERP are deep; switching costs compound over time. **Conclusion: Renew. Confidence: 80.** Integration dependency is a hard constraint, not a soft preference.

**Weighted final recommendation:** Renew — but negotiate. Paths 1 and 4 have highest combined confidence (75 + 80 = 155 vs 60 + 55 = 115 for migrate paths). Financial savings are real but Path 4's lock-in risk is the deciding factor. Recommended action: Use migration option as negotiating leverage to reduce increase from 40% to 15-20%.

### Gotchas

AI confidence scores are not calibrated probabilities — they reflect the model's internal consistency, not real-world accuracy. Treat them as relative weights, not absolute reliability metrics. If all four paths return confidence below 60, that is itself a signal: the decision has genuine uncertainty that AI cannot resolve; you need additional data before deciding.

### Key takeaways

CISC gives you something single-answer prompts never can: a transparent view of which reasoning holds up most robustly and which depends on uncertain assumptions. For executives, this mirrors the way good management consultants structure options analysis — multiple perspectives, explicit assumptions, ranked confidence. The technique also surfaces when a decision is genuinely too uncertain to make without more information.

## Exercise 23: Prompt Repetition

### Theoretical background

Prompt Repetition is the simplest advanced technique discovered in 2025: paste your question twice in the same prompt (`<question><question>`). Decoder-only language models process text sequentially. When the model reaches the second instance of the question, it has already fully processed the first — creating a pseudo-bidirectional context effect that reduces the chance of missing key details in complex queries.

**Evidence:** Up to 76% accuracy improvement on non-reasoning tasks in tests by Google Research.
**Source:** [arXiv:2512.14982](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.14982), December 2025, Google Research.

**Important caveat:** Prompt repetition doubles input tokens, which doubles cost on token-based APIs. Use only for short, precise, high-importance tasks — not for long document analysis or RAG-based queries where context is already large.

### Student task

Select a precise, high-stakes question where getting all the nuance right matters — a strategic question, a decision brief, a risk identification task. Test it with and without repetition. Observe whether the repeated version catches subtleties the single version missed.

### Examples

**Single question (standard):**
"What are the key risks in acquiring a professional services firm with 40% revenue concentration in one client?"

**With Prompt Repetition:**
```
What are the key risks in acquiring a professional services firm with
40% revenue concentration in one client?

What are the key risks in acquiring a professional services firm with
40% revenue concentration in one client?
```

**Observed difference:** Single-question response typically covers: client loss risk, valuation impact, earn-out structure. Repeated version additionally surfaced: key-person dependency (the relationship owner for that client), restrictive covenant limitations, and the due diligence signal that 40% concentration implies failed business development — a structural problem, not just a client risk.

**Executive use case — precision document review:**
```
Review the following contract clause and identify any terms that could create
liability exposure for us as the service provider:

[clause text]

Review the following contract clause and identify any terms that could create
liability exposure for us as the service provider:

[clause text]
```

The repetition increases the probability the model identifies subtle or embedded liabilities that a single pass might deprioritise in favour of obvious ones.

### Gotchas

Do not use for long document analysis, RAG pipelines, or any prompt where context already exceeds 2,000 tokens — the cost doubles without proportional benefit. This technique works best for short, focused, high-precision queries. Also note: this applies to non-reasoning models (GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet). For reasoning models (o3, Claude Extended Thinking), the internal multi-pass reasoning already achieves a similar effect.

### Key takeaways

Prompt repetition is the lowest-effort high-return technique in this guide. It requires no structural change to your thinking or prompt design — just paste twice. For executives who send important queries once and trust the answer, adding a single repetition is a near-zero-cost accuracy upgrade on precision tasks.

## Exercise 24: Adversarial Chain-of-Thought

### Theoretical background

Adversarial Chain-of-Thought (Adv-CoT) embeds a prompt improvement loop within a single prompt: a generator proposes reasoning, a discriminator identifies failure cases, and revisions are made iteratively — all in one AI interaction. Rather than you iterating on the prompt manually across sessions, the model does it internally.

**Evidence:** Average +4.44% accuracy improvement across 12 reasoning datasets with GPT-3.5-turbo; improvements on sports reasoning (+4.5%), arithmetic (+3.7%), and algebraic reasoning (+3.9%). Low variance across repeated runs confirms stable gains.
**Source:** [MDPI Information, December 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2078-2489/16/12/1092).

For executives, Adv-CoT is most valuable for refining prompts you plan to reuse — sales qualification criteria, document review checklists, decision frameworks. Run it once to improve the prompt, then use the improved version repeatedly.

### Student task

Identify a prompt you use regularly but feel produces inconsistent or incomplete results. Write an Adv-CoT meta-prompt asking the model to: (1) identify 3 failure cases in the original prompt, (2) revise the prompt to address each, (3) generate a response with the improved prompt, (4) explain what improved. Compare original and improved outputs.

### Examples

**Original prompt (used repeatedly, produces variable results):**
"Review this proposal and tell me if it's ready to send to the client."

**Adv-CoT improvement prompt:**
```
I have this prompt I use to review client proposals:
"Review this proposal and tell me if it's ready to send to the client."

Please do the following:
1. Identify 3 specific failure cases — situations where this prompt would
   produce incomplete, misleading, or unhelpful responses.
2. Rewrite the prompt to prevent each failure case.
3. Show me what a response to the improved prompt looks like, applied to
   a hypothetical proposal for a €200K AI training engagement.
4. Explain concisely how the improved version is better.
```

**AI output:**

*Failure case 1:* "Ready to send" is undefined. The model has no criteria — it will default to surface-level grammar/formatting checks and miss strategic or commercial gaps.

*Failure case 2:* No client context provided. A proposal for a risk-averse public sector client requires different readiness criteria than one for a startup CEO.

*Failure case 3:* No reviewer perspective specified. The model doesn't know if it should review as a senior partner, a client procurement officer, or a legal reviewer.

*Improved prompt:*
```
Review the following client proposal against these specific criteria:
1. Executive summary: Does it state the problem, solution, and value in under 150 words?
2. Commercial clarity: Is pricing justified with clear ROI or reference to benchmarks?
3. Risk mitigation: Are client concerns (timeline, delivery risk, dependencies) addressed?
4. Call to action: Is the next step clear and time-bound?
5. Tone calibration: Is the language appropriate for [client type: e.g. CFO of a mid-cap manufacturer]?

Flag any section that would cause a senior client stakeholder to hesitate or ask a clarifying question before signing.
```

*Sample output on improved prompt:* [Detailed section-by-section review against criteria, with specific line-level flags.]

### Gotchas

Adv-CoT produces longer outputs and works best when your original prompt is specific enough to have identifiable failure modes. If your original prompt is too vague ("make this better"), the model cannot identify meaningful failure cases. Start with a prompt you already use in practice, not a hypothetical one. The improved prompt from Adv-CoT should be saved and used going forward — the value compounds when the better prompt becomes your standard.

### Key takeaways

Adv-CoT turns prompt improvement from a manual trial-and-error process into a structured, single-session exercise. For executives who use AI for recurring tasks (proposal review, meeting prep, decision briefs), running Adv-CoT once on your standard prompts and saving the result is one of the highest-leverage investments of AI prompting time. The improved prompts become institutional assets.

## Exercise 25: DR-CoT (Dynamic Recursive Chain of Thought)

### Theoretical background

DR-CoT addresses two weaknesses of standard Chain-of-Thought for long or complex reasoning tasks: (1) context dilution — important early reasoning gets "forgotten" as the chain grows long, and (2) high token costs. DR-CoT combines three mechanisms: recursive sub-problem decomposition, dynamic context pruning (keeping only essential content within a fixed token budget per step), and multi-path voting to verify the final answer.

**Evidence:** Consistently 3-4 percentage points higher than standard CoT on AIME 2024 (mathematics competition). On GPQA Diamond, small BERT-class models using DR-CoT outperformed GPT-4 and LLaMA 2 on zero-shot basis.
**Source:** [Scientific Reports / Nature, Vol. 15, 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-18622-6).

For executives, DR-CoT is most relevant for: complex financial modelling analysis, multi-step scenario planning, long regulatory or contract analysis, or any task where reasoning chains span many intermediate steps and accuracy is critical.

### Student task

Select a problem requiring more than 4-5 reasoning steps where accuracy at each step affects later conclusions (compound reasoning). Write a DR-CoT prompt specifying: sub-problem decomposition, a token/length budget per step, two independent solution paths, and reconciliation of those paths before giving the final answer.

### Examples

**Problem:** Evaluate whether a 5-year managed services contract at current terms generates positive NPV given projected cost inflation.

**Standard CoT prompt:**
"Calculate the NPV of a 5-year managed services contract. Revenue: €500K/year. Costs grow at 8%/year. Discount rate: 10%. Starting cost base: €300K."

**AI response:** Often produces correct Year 1, then compounds errors across years due to context dilution of the base figure.

**DR-CoT prompt:**
```
Evaluate the NPV of the following managed services contract. Follow these rules:

Rules:
- Decompose into annual sub-calculations (Year 1 through Year 5).
- At each year, reference only the immediately prior year's output
  (do not carry forward the full calculation chain).
- Limit each year's working to the essential figures only
  (token budget: max 3 lines per year).
- Solve twice using two independent methods:
  Path A: Year-by-year cumulative.
  Path B: Closed-form PV formula.
- If results match within 5%, present as confirmed answer.
  If they diverge, flag the discrepancy and show both.

Contract details:
- Revenue: €500K/year (fixed)
- Cost base Year 1: €300K, growing 8%/year
- Discount rate: 10%
- Contract length: 5 years
```

**AI response (DR-CoT):**

*Year 1:* Revenue €500K, Cost €300K, Margin €200K. PV = €200K / 1.10 = €181,818
*Year 2:* Cost €324K, Margin €176K. PV = €176K / 1.21 = €145,455
*Year 3:* Cost €349.9K, Margin €150.1K. PV = €150.1K / 1.331 = €112,772
*Year 4:* Cost €377.9K, Margin €122.1K. PV = €122.1K / 1.464 = €83,402
*Year 5:* Cost €408.1K, Margin €91.9K. PV = €91.9K / 1.611 = €57,047

*Path A total NPV: €580,494*

*Path B (formula verification):* [closed-form calculation] = €578,900

*Variance: €1,594 (0.27%) — within 5% threshold.*
**Confirmed NPV: ~€580K. Contract generates positive returns. Recommend proceed.**

### Gotchas

DR-CoT requires you to specify the token budget and independent paths clearly — vague instructions produce standard CoT without the pruning benefit. The two-path verification is the most important element: if paths diverge by more than your threshold, that is a signal to investigate which assumption is wrong before trusting the answer. Do not use DR-CoT for simple calculations where standard arithmetic suffices; reserve it for multi-step compound reasoning where intermediate error propagation is a real risk.

### Key takeaways

DR-CoT is the most technically rigorous prompting technique in this guide. It imports from software engineering (modular, stateless computation with verification) into language model reasoning. For executives who use AI for financial analysis, scenario modelling, or complex contract evaluation, DR-CoT provides a structured audit trail and built-in error detection. The two-path verification step alone — asking AI to confirm its answer via an independent method — should become standard practice for any high-stakes numerical output.

## Appendix: Techniques to Avoid in 2026

This appendix documents prompting approaches that were effective on earlier AI models (2022-2024) but have been confirmed less effective or counterproductive on current frontier models (GPT-4o, Claude 3.5/3.7, Gemini Advanced and above). Knowing what *not* to do is as important as knowing what works.

### 1. "Think step by step" on Reasoning Models

**What it is:** Explicitly instructing CoT on models that already reason internally (OpenAI o3/o4-mini, Claude Extended Thinking, Gemini Thinking).

**Why it's now ineffective:** These models perform multi-step reasoning by default. Adding explicit CoT instruction is redundant and increases response time by 20-80% with no accuracy gain.

**Source:** [arXiv:2506.07142](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.07142), Wharton Generative AI Labs, June 2025.

**Correct approach:** State clearly what result you want. Let reasoning models reason. Reserve explicit CoT for non-reasoning models (GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet without Extended Thinking enabled).

---

### 2. Role Prompting for Knowledge Expansion

**What it is:** "You are a world-class expert with 20 years of experience in X" — used to get more accurate or deeper factual information.

**Why it's now ineffective:** Confirmed not to expand knowledge boundaries. The model knows what it knows regardless of persona. Worse, role prompting can amplify biases associated with that persona archetype.

**Source:** [arXiv:2409.13979](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.13979v2), updated February 2025.

**Still effective for:** Tone control, output format compliance, communication style. "Review this briefly like a grumpy senior engineer" or "Respond only in JSON format as an API server" remain valid uses.

---

### 3. More Than 5 Few-Shot Examples

**What it is:** Providing 6, 8, or 10 examples to ensure the model understands the pattern.

**Why it's now counterproductive:** "Few-Shot Collapse" confirmed — performance drops sharply beyond 4-5 examples. Models overfit to the demonstrated patterns and lose generalisation. Gemini Flash on path optimisation: 0-shot 33% → 4-shot 64% → 8-shot back to 33%.

**Source:** [arXiv:2509.13196](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.13196), September 2025.

**Correct approach:** 2-3 high-quality, diverse examples. Examples are for format and pattern demonstration, not for teaching the model what it already knows.

---

### 4. Complex Scaffolding on Frontier Models

**What it is:** Elaborate step-by-step instruction sequences ("First do X, then Y, then Z, then review, then...") applied to GPT-5 or Claude Opus-class models.

**Why it's now counterproductive:** "Prompting Inversion" phenomenon — complex constraints force excessive literal interpretation on high-capability models, blocking autonomous reasoning. GPT-5 Zero-Shot already exceeds the best GPT-4o prompted results on major benchmarks.

**Source:** [arXiv:2510.22251](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.22251), October 2025.

**Correct approach:** For frontier models, state clearly what you want as the outcome. For GPT-4o-level models, structured scaffolding still helps. Know which model you are using and calibrate accordingly.

---

### 5. Emotional Manipulation Phrases

**What it is:** "I'll give you a $200 tip," "I'll get fired if you don't help," "This is critical for my career," "Please, I'm begging you."

**Why it's now ineffective:** Confirmed no consistent measurable effect on modern frontier models. Early EmotionPrompt research (2023) showed gains on older, smaller models; these do not replicate on current frontier systems.

**Source:** Wharton GAIL Prompting Science Report 2, 2025; Medium "Magic Phrases Don't Work," January 2026.

**Correct approach:** Provide specific context — data scale, current problem, target metrics, environment. "Our orders table has 5M rows, query takes 5 seconds, target is under 500ms on PostgreSQL 15" outperforms any emotional appeal by orders of magnitude.
